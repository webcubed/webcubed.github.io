The Coriolis effect exists because the Earth is rotating. The Earth's rotation causes a force to act on objects moving on the Earth's surface. This force is called the Coriolis force. The Coriolis force deflects objects to the right in the Northern Hemisphere and to the left in the Southern Hemisphere.
The Coriolis force is caused by the Earth's rotation. As an object moves on the Earth's surface, it is constantly moving relative to the Earth's rotating frame of reference. This movement causes the object to experience a force that is perpendicular to its direction of motion. This force is the Coriolis force.
The Coriolis force is strongest at the equator and weakest at the poles. This is because the Earth's rotation is fastest at the equator and slowest at the poles.
