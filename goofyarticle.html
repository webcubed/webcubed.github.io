In a far-off future, Satch and Riv are teenagers tasked with gathering food for their group. Riv has discovered the remains of a house, and she is taking Satch to see it for himself. The Relic “I don’t believe you.” “You don’t gotta believe me—it’s still true.” Satch rolled his eyes. Riv was easily the stubbornest person he knew. Normally, that was why he liked to go foraging with her—she wouldn’t quit until she’d culled every last berry, rooted out every hidden mushroom. Stubborn survives, Gramma Jo liked to intone—and Riv’s stubbornness did help them all survive. But when something other than foraging drew Riv’s interest, nothing could deter her. “But how can a house talk?” Satch repeated, struggling to keep up on the rocky hillside. --- Can’t cook? With the new all-in-one Mixit, you can, and fast! It chops, cubes, minces—even handles those tricky julienne cuts! --- Ahead, intrepid Riv led the way, a dark stain of sweat on her back despite the shade of the overgrown trees. They had left the path half an hour ago and were squandering precious foraging time, Satch thought. “You must have just heard folks talking and thought—” “It wasn’t folks,” said Riv, “’cause you can tell when there’s folks around, and there wasn’t. There was music too, and light like when the sun hits the water, and—” “And the house was talking,” sighed Satch, feeling his pack chafe the skin on his shoulders. --- Tired of keeping track of different sizes of containers and the tops to fit them all? Try Osfall, the one-size storage solution! --- Riv stopped abruptly at the crest of a hill, and Satch felt his skin crinkle with a shiver unrelated to the sweat now cooling on his back. Below them was the ruined husk of a house, now broken-down and claimed by vines. Atop what was left of the roof, smooth panels gleamed in the afternoon sun, making sharp diamonds of blue-black light. --- My guest today is Sylvia, who feels that today’s teens spend too much time with technology. Everyone playing along at home, press the heart button to welcome Sylvia! --- “It’s bigger than it looked from up there,” said Satch apprehensively. “Folks used to live big, Gramma Jo says—too big and too fast.” Satch imagined her voice: Slow down, pay attention, Gramma Jo would speak in a low timbre. Before he could repeat the words, Riv held up her hand, cueing him to listen: voices murmured inside, sounding flattened somehow, as though they were distanced from whomever was speaking. Following Riv’s gaze, Satch saw light, shifting and playing. In a rectangular box on the wall, pictures of people flickered, bright and flat. Wearing clothes from Before and talking as though unaware they could be seen, they chattered and argued, interrupting, and invisible others laughed and whooped. Music smashed in—no instrument Satch recognized—and all at once the people were replaced by flashing colors and words, each slicing away before Satch could make sense of it. “Could folks read that fast back then?” Riv didn’t answer; she was creeping closer to the wall, transfixed, as a man bellowed—Hurry! sale ends Sunday!—so loudly that Satch jumped back. Suddenly two cats appeared, bouncing their heads in sync in a way Satch had never seen cats move. “Riv,” said Satch, patting her shoulder, “we’re losing time and we should leave—Riv!” At Satch’s touch she turned at last, and her eyes took a moment to focus, as though she were waking from a dream. “Slow down. Pay attention,” Riv whispered. “This is what Gramma Jo means—it must have been like this all the time Before.” She shivered, speaking more forcefully: “You’re right, Satch, we should go—now.”
